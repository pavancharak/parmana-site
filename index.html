The gap

AI can act. But who authorized it?

Organizations can define policies, manage identities, and log actions after the fact. What they can't do today is verify — before execution that a specific autonomous action was explicitly authorized.

Policy exists. Enforcement doesn't.

You have approval workflows and governance policies. But when an AI agent triggers a payment or procurement action, there is no layer that checks authorization before execution happens.

Human approval doesn't scale.

At low volumes, human-in-the-loop works. At scale, it becomes a bottleneck adding latency, cost, and human error to every automated action.

Logs aren't proof.

Audit logs tell you what happened. They don't prove the action was authorized at the moment of execution, or that the record hasn't been altered since.

Regulators are watching.

RBI, IRDAI, and SEBI are moving toward mandating explainability for automated decisions. "The AI did it" is not a compliance answer.

The trust gap

The missing layer between AI and execution

Today's stack goes from policy definition straight to action execution. The enforcement layer the one that verifies authority in real time and creates independent proof doesn't exist yet. That's what Parmana builds.

Separation

AI does not authorize itself

The agent that requests an action is never the authority that approves it. Hard architectural separation enforced at the infrastructure level.

Independence

Proof lives outside the system

Attestations are stored independently. Verification requires no access to the system that created the decision.

Scale

Define once, enforce always

Authority rules are written by humans once. Parmana enforces them automatically across every subsequent action no bottlenecks.
